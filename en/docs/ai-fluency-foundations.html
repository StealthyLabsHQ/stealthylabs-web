1 Understanding Generative AI

Generative AI refers to artificial intelligence systems that can create new content rather than just analyzing existing data. Where traditional AI might classify an email as spam or not spam, generative AI can write a completely new email for you.

Three converging developments made this possible:

Pillar What happened
Algorithms The Transformer architecture (2017) revolutionized processing of long text passages by letting models pay attention to relationships between words across entire passages at once.
Data explosion The internet produced massive amounts of digital text (websites, books, code repositories) that provided raw material for training.
Computing power GPU advances made it feasible to train large models on billions of examples within a reasonable timeframe.

How it works

1
Pre-training – The model analyzes billions of text examples, learning to predict what word or token comes next. This builds broad language understanding and world knowledge.
2
Fine-tuning – The model is refined to follow instructions, be helpful, and avoid generating harmful content. This is where models like Claude develop their conversational behavior.
3
Deployment – Users provide prompts, and the model generates responses based on both the prompt and the patterns it learned during training.

Key capabilities

  • Versatile language skills – writing, summarizing, translating, explaining
  • General-purpose reasoning across many domains
  • Learning from examples provided in the conversation
  • Connecting to external tools and data sources

Current limitations

  • Knowledge cutoff date – No built-in awareness of events after training ended
  • Hallucinations – Can confidently state things that are plausible but incorrect
  • Context window constraints – Can only consider a finite amount of text at once
  • Complex reasoning and math – Still prone to errors on multi-step logical problems

2 The 4D Framework

AI Fluency is organized around four interconnected competencies. Together they ensure your interactions with AI are effective, efficient, ethical, and safe.

Competency Core question What it covers
Delegation "Should I involve AI, and how?" Deciding what work is appropriate for humans, AI, or both – and distributing tasks accordingly.
Description "Have I communicated clearly?" Effectively describing goals, processes, and expectations to prompt useful behaviors and outputs.
Discernment "Is what I got actually good?" Thoughtfully and critically evaluating AI outputs, reasoning, and behavior.
Diligence "Am I being responsible?" Taking accountability for what you do with AI and how you do it.
These four competencies are not a checklist to complete in order. They work as a continuous feedback loop – discernment informs better description, which improves delegation, and so on.

3 Delegation

Delegation means making thoughtful decisions about what work is appropriate for you to do, for AI to do, or for you and AI to do together – and how to distribute those tasks. Effective delegation requires both domain expertise and an understanding of AI capabilities.

Problem Awareness

Clearly understanding your goals and the nature of the work before involving AI. Ask yourself: "What am I actually trying to accomplish?" Jumping to AI without a clear goal leads to vague outputs that require extensive revision.

  • Define the specific outcome you want
  • Identify what kind of task it is (creative, analytical, informational, etc.)
  • Consider whether AI involvement adds value or creates risk

Platform Awareness

Understanding the capabilities and limitations of different AI systems. Not every AI tool is suited for every task. Ask: "What does this system do well?"

  • Know the model's strengths (reasoning, coding, writing, summarizing)
  • Know its limitations (knowledge cutoff, hallucination risk, context window)
  • Choose the right tool for the job

Task Delegation

Thoughtfully distributing work between humans and AI to leverage the strengths of each. Ask: "What's the plan for who does what?"

  • Give AI tasks where it excels: summarizing, drafting, transforming formats
  • Keep human tasks where judgment, ethics, and accountability matter
  • Design the collaboration deliberately, not by default

4 Description

Description is the ability to communicate with AI in ways that create a productive collaborative environment. Clear communication up front saves time and leads to better results. It has three dimensions:

Product Description

Defining what you want in terms of outputs, format, audience, and style. Without this, AI will guess – and it will often guess wrong.

  • Specify the format: bullet list, essay, table, code snippet, etc.
  • Define the audience: expert, beginner, executive, student, etc.
  • Set the tone and style: formal, casual, concise, comprehensive
  • State the length: two paragraphs, 500 words, a single sentence

Process Description

Defining how the AI should approach your request. Providing step-by-step instructions guides the AI's reasoning and reduces the chance of it taking shortcuts.

  • "First analyze X, then compare with Y, then recommend Z"
  • Ask the AI to work through its reasoning before answering
  • Break multi-step problems into explicit phases

Performance Description

Defining how the AI behaves during your collaboration: whether it should be concise or detailed, challenging or supportive, creative or conservative.

  • "Act as a Socratic tutor – ask questions rather than giving answers"
  • "Be concise and direct. No preamble."
  • "Push back if you think my reasoning is flawed"

5 Discernment

Discernment is the ability to thoughtfully and critically evaluate what AI produces, how it produces it, and how it behaves. Discernment works hand-in-hand with Description in a continuous feedback loop – what you observe informs how you refine your prompts.

Product Discernment

Evaluating the quality of what AI produces. Check for:

  • Accuracy – Is it factually correct? Can you verify key claims?
  • Appropriateness – Is it suitable for the intended audience and context?
  • Coherence – Does it hold together logically from start to finish?
  • Relevance – Does it actually address what you asked?

Process Discernment

Evaluating how the AI arrived at its output. Look for:

  • Logical errors or jumps in reasoning
  • Lapses in attention (did it address all parts of the prompt?)
  • Inappropriate reasoning steps (did it make assumptions it shouldn't?)

Performance Discernment

Evaluating how the AI behaves during your interaction. Ask:

  • Is the communication style effective for what you need right now?
  • Is it being overly agreeable or pushing back when it should?
  • Is the level of detail appropriate?
The Description-Discernment loop: When discernment reveals a problem, the fix is almost always better description. Refine your prompt, re-run, re-evaluate. This loop is the core practice of AI Fluency.

6 Diligence

Diligence means taking responsibility for what you do with AI and how you do it. AI can assist – but you remain accountable for the outcome. Different contexts (personal, academic, professional) may have different expectations for disclosure and verification.

Creation Diligence

Being thoughtful about which AI systems you use and how you interact with them.

  • Choose tools that are appropriate for the sensitivity of the task
  • Understand what data you are sharing and with whom
  • Document which AI model was used and when, when it matters (e.g., "Claude 3.7, March 2026")

Transparency Diligence

Being honest about AI's role in your work with everyone who needs to know.

  • Disclose AI assistance in academic, professional, or public contexts
  • Don't present AI-generated work as entirely your own when transparency is expected
  • Help others understand how AI contributed to a result

Deployment Diligence

Taking responsibility for verifying and vouching for the outputs you use or share.

  • Don't deploy AI output without reviewing it yourself
  • You are responsible for the final product, not the AI
  • If something is wrong in what you shared, you own the correction

7 Interaction Modes

Human-AI collaboration takes three fundamental forms. Understanding which mode you are in helps you set the right expectations and apply the right delegation strategy.

Mode What it means Example
Automation AI executes specific tasks based on human instructions. Human defines what needs to be done; AI does it. "Translate this document to Spanish."
Augmentation Humans and AI collaborate as thinking partners. Iterative back-and-forth where both contribute to the outcome. Drafting a report together, refining it across multiple exchanges.
Agency Humans configure AI to work independently on their behalf, including interacting with other systems. Human sets the AI's knowledge and behavior; AI acts within that framework. An AI agent that monitors your inbox and drafts replies based on your instructions.

8 6 Prompting Techniques

Effective prompting is a learnable skill. These six techniques directly apply the Description competency and will improve your results immediately.

1. Provide context

Be specific about what you want, providing details about scope, geography, timeframe, and other relevant parameters.

BeforeAfter
Tell me about climate change. Explain three major impacts of climate change on agriculture in tropical regions, with examples from the past decade.

2. Show examples of what "good" looks like

Providing examples helps the AI understand the pattern, style, or format you're looking for more clearly than descriptions alone. This is called few-shot prompting.

Give 1-3 examples of the desired input-output pattern before your actual request. The AI will match the style and format of your examples.

3. Specify output constraints

Be explicit about format, length, structure, or other characteristics of the expected response.

BeforeAfter
Design me a personal art portfolio website. Create a clean, modern single-page portfolio website with these sections: Hero, About Me, Skills, Portfolio, Experience, and Contact. Navigation must be sticky and responsive, with a hamburger menu on mobile. Use a sunset color palette and add a dark/light mode toggle.

4. Break complex tasks into steps

Breaking down complex tasks into clear steps guides the AI's reasoning and ensures thorough, methodical responses.

Instead of "Analyze this sales data", try: "1. Identify top-performing products. 2. Compare this quarter to last quarter. 3. Highlight unusual patterns. 4. Suggest possible reasons."

5. Ask it to think first

Giving the AI space to reason before responding encourages more thoughtful, comprehensive answers. Explicitly say: "Before answering, think through the different factors, potential constraints, and approaches."

6. Define the AI's role

Defining a role, tone, or persona shapes the AI's approach to fit your specific needs.

Example: "Explain how rainbows form from the perspective of an experienced science teacher speaking to a bright 10-year-old."

Secret weapon: ask AI to help you prompt

When you're not sure how to phrase your request, ask Claude directly:

"I'm trying to get you to help me with [goal]. I'm not sure how to phrase my request to get the best results. Can you help me craft an effective prompt?"

9 Vocabulary Reference

Key terms organized by category.

AI Systems

TermDefinition
Generative AIAI systems that create new content (text, images, code) rather than just analyzing existing data.
Large Language Model (LLM)Generative AI trained on vast amounts of text to understand and generate human language.
ClaudeAnthropic's family of large language models.
Neural networksComputing systems composed of interconnected nodes organized in layers that learn patterns from data.
Transformer architectureThe 2017 AI design breakthrough that enables LLMs to process text in parallel while tracking relationships between words across long passages.
ParametersThe mathematical values within a model that determine how it processes and relates information. Modern LLMs contain billions.

Training & Behavior

TermDefinition
Pre-trainingInitial training phase where models learn language patterns from vast text data.
Fine-tuningAdditional training to follow instructions, give helpful responses, and avoid harmful content.
Scaling lawsThe empirical observation that model performance improves consistently as size and training data grow, sometimes unlocking entirely new capabilities.
HallucinationWhen AI confidently states something plausible but factually incorrect.
Knowledge cutoff dateThe point after which an AI model has no built-in knowledge, based on when training ended.
Context windowThe maximum amount of information an AI can consider at one time, including conversation history and shared documents.
TemperatureA setting controlling response randomness. Higher = more creative and varied. Lower = more focused and predictable.
BiasSystematic patterns in AI outputs that unfairly favor or disadvantage certain groups, often reflecting training data imbalances.
Retrieval Augmented Generation (RAG)A technique connecting AI to external knowledge sources to improve accuracy and reduce hallucinations.
Reasoning modelsAI models designed to think step-by-step through complex problems, showing improved logical reasoning capabilities.

Prompt Engineering

TermDefinition
PromptThe input given to an AI model, including instructions and any documents shared.
Prompt engineeringThe practice of designing effective prompts to produce desired outputs.
Chain-of-thought promptingEncouraging AI to work through a problem step by step before answering.
Few-shot learningTeaching AI by showing examples of the desired input-output pattern. "N-shot" = N examples provided.
Role / persona definitionSpecifying a character, expertise level, or communication style for the AI to adopt.
Output constraintsSpecifying desired format, length, structure, or other characteristics of the AI's response.
Think-first approachExplicitly asking AI to work through its reasoning before providing a final answer.
